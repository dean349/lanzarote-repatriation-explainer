I, Philip Harrison, am writing this email to myself as a contemporaneous personal record of a serious financial irregularity that I wish to document formally.

**Background**

I am the sole director and a 50% shareholder of **Los Romeros Limited** (Company Registration Number: 06993349), a UK-registered limited company. The company's principal asset was a residential property located in **Lanzarote, Canary Islands, Spain** — specifically the villa at Calle Los Roques 9, Playa Blanca.

Wincham Accountants (and their successor firm, Adrem Accounting) have acted as company secretary and accountants for Los Romeros Limited throughout the period of my ownership. They arranged and managed all aspects of the company's administration, including the process of selling the Lanzarote property.

**The Sale**

The Lanzarote property was sold by Los Romeros Limited at a Spanish notary on approximately **20 March 2026**. The gross sale price was **€315,000**. At the point of completion, a mandatory **3% AEAT (Spanish tax authority) retention of approximately €9,450** was withheld from the gross proceeds under Modelo 211, leaving net proceeds of approximately **€305,550** payable to the company.

These net proceeds are the assets of **Los Romeros Limited**, a separate legal entity from me as an individual. The money belonged to the company, not to me personally.

**The Banking Error**

Prior to completion, I was directed by Wincham / Adrem to provide a personal UK bank account number to the Spanish notary or their associated currency exchange intermediary. I provided my personal HSBC bank account details as instructed.

As a result, on or around **27 March 2026**, the net sale proceeds (approximately **€305,550**, which was converted to approximately **£258,000–£265,000 GBP** by the currency exchange firm engaged as part of the process) were deposited directly into **my personal HSBC bank account**.

At no point was I advised by Wincham or Adrem that:

1. The proceeds were the legal property of **Los Romeros Limited**, not my personal funds;
2. Depositing corporate proceeds into a personal account without a formal Director's Loan Agreement creates a liability under **HMRC Section 455 Corporation Tax Act 2010**, potentially triggering a 33.75% tax charge on the company;
3. I should have opened or used a **corporate business bank account** in the name of Los Romeros Limited to receive the proceeds; or
4. The absence of a corporate account, and the misdirection of funds to my personal HSBC account, would significantly complicate the process of winding up the company via a **Members' Voluntary Liquidation (MVL)**.

**What Should Have Happened**

The correct procedure — which Wincham / Adrem, as the company's professional advisers and company secretary, were responsible for ensuring — was that the net sale proceeds should have been received into a **UK business bank account held in the name of Los Romeros Limited**. From there, the funds would have been properly dealt with through either:

- A formal distribution to shareholders following settlement of the company's liabilities (including Spanish CGT, UK Corporation Tax, and any MVL costs); or
- A formal Director's Loan arrangement, documented and reported to HMRC.

Neither of these steps was taken. No business bank account was ever opened for Los Romeros Limited. No Director's Loan Agreement was ever put in place. No advice was ever given to me about the tax consequences of receiving the proceeds personally.

**Impact**

As a direct result of the misdirection of funds, I now face:

1. A potential **HMRC Section 455 tax liability** on the approximately £258,000–£265,000 sitting in my personal HSBC account, until a proper Director's Loan Agreement is backdated and formally acknowledged;
2. Significantly increased complexity and cost in completing the MVL, because there are no corporate bank accounts to transfer through;
3. The risk that HMRC may treat the corporate proceeds as a **personal distribution** rather than a capital receipt, resulting in a substantially higher Income Tax / Dividend Tax rate rather than the 18–24% CGT rate that would apply through a properly conducted MVL;
4. A formal claim for professional negligence and breach of fiduciary duty against Wincham Accountants and/or Adrem Accounting, which I intend to pursue.
