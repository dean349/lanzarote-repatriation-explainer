We write on behalf of Philip Harrison ("the Claimant") in relation to professional negligence, breach of fiduciary duty, and breach of contract arising from the conduct of Wincham Accountants Limited, Wincham International Limited, and associated entities within the Wincham Group ("the Defendants") in connection with the administration of Los Romeros Limited, a UK limited company incorporated at the Defendants' direction for the purpose of holding the Claimant's Spanish property in Lanzarote, Canary Islands.

The Claimant has suffered quantifiable financial loss and has been exposed to ongoing adverse tax and regulatory consequences as a direct result of the Defendants' failures. This letter sets out the facts, the particulars of breach, the loss suffered, and the remedies sought.

1. The Parties

The Claimant: Philip Harrison, a retired UK resident domiciled in Sheffield, England. The Claimant is a consumer and lay person with no specialist knowledge of corporate law, Spanish property tax, or UK inheritance tax planning. He placed complete reliance upon the Defendants as specialist professional advisers.

The Defendants: The Wincham Group of Companies, operating since approximately 1994, including at minimum Wincham International Limited and Wincham Accountants Limited, directed by Malcolm Roach and Mark Roach, with Michael Ball FCA named as accountancy lead. The Defendants have at all material times held themselves out as specialists in Spanish property ownership through UK limited company structures, claiming membership of AIPP, an HMRC MLR Licence (No. 12606134), and professional indemnity insurance cover.

3. Particulars of Negligence and Breach of Duty

The Defendants owed the Claimant a duty of care in tort pursuant to the principles established in Caparo Industries plc v Dickman [1990] 2 AC 605 and Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller & Partners Ltd [1964] AC 465, and implied contractual duties of reasonable care and skill pursuant to section 13 of the Supply of Goods and Services Act 1982. The Defendants breached those duties in the following material particulars:

3.1
Failure to advise on the necessity of a business bank account (Primary Breach)
The Defendants incorporated Los Romeros Limited, acted as company secretary, filed annual accounts, and administered the company throughout the engagement. They knew or ought to have known that the eventual purpose of the company was to hold and dispose of the Property and receive the resulting Proceeds. The Defendants failed entirely to advise the Claimant at any stage that Los Romeros Limited required a business bank account. This omission constitutes a fundamental breach of the duty to advise the Claimant on all matters necessary for the proper functioning of the corporate structure they had created.
3.2
Unauthorised receipt and control of client funds — RBC Client Account
The Defendants caused or permitted the Proceeds (approximately €315,000) to be transmitted to their own RBC client account without the Claimant's explicit, informed written authorisation to hold those funds. By accepting custody of the Proceeds, the Defendants assumed fiduciary obligations in respect of the funds. In holding client money of this magnitude, the Defendants were required to comply with the ICAEW Client Money regulations and the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (SI 2017/692). The Claimant will require disclosure of all documentation relating to the authorisation, opening, receipts, and disbursements of the RBC client account.
3.3
Misdirection of Proceeds to Claimant's personal account
The Defendants, having assumed custody of the Proceeds, disbursed those funds to the Claimant's personal HSBC bank account rather than to Los Romeros Limited. This was a direct breach of the Defendants' duty to exercise reasonable skill and care in administering the corporate structure. The correct recipient of the Proceeds was Los Romeros Limited. The misdirection to a personal account creates potential adverse tax consequences including but not limited to: income tax treatment of a distribution; loss of the preferential Members' Voluntary Liquidation capital treatment; and HMRC scrutiny of the transaction structure.
3.4
Misrepresentation of ITP tax savings applicable to Lanzarote
The Defendants' marketing materials published between 2008 and 2026 across multiple domains (wincham.com, winchamukcompany.com) represented that the UK limited company structure would remove or reduce Spanish Impuesto sobre Transmisiones Patrimoniales ("ITP") of "7%" or "7–10%." The applicable ITP rate for residential property in the Canary Islands (including Lanzarote) was 6.5% at the material times — below the lowest figure quoted by the Defendants. This constitutes a negligent misstatement under Hedley Byrne and a misrepresentation under section 2 of the Misrepresentation Act 1967, inducing the Claimant to enter into the arrangement on an inaccurate basis.
3.5
Failure to maintain the corporate structure in a legally functioning condition
A UK limited company which does not have a business bank account is not capable of performing its primary commercial function — namely receiving, holding, and distributing funds. The Defendants, as company administrator and de facto corporate secretary, owed a duty to ensure that Los Romeros Limited was maintained in a state capable of fulfilling its purpose. Their failure to do so rendered the structure inherently defective from its inception.
3.6
Potential breach of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017
The routing of property sale proceeds of approximately €315,000 through the Defendants' own RBC client account, followed by disbursement to a personal account rather than the corporate entity which legally owned the property, constitutes a transaction structure which triggers enhanced due diligence obligations under Regulation 33 of the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 (SI 2017/692). The Claimant will require confirmation of whether the Defendants complied with their AML obligations in respect of this transaction and whether any Suspicious Activity Report ("SAR") was filed.

4. Causation

The Claimant's losses (set out at Section 5 below) flow directly and foreseeably from the Defendants' breaches. But for the Defendants' failure to advise on the requirement for a business bank account (breach 3.1), the Proceeds would have been received by Los Romeros Limited into its own business account, enabling the company to proceed to a Members' Voluntary Liquidation with full capital gains tax treatment at the applicable Entrepreneurs' Relief or Business Asset Disposal Relief rate. The misdirection of funds to the Claimant's personal account has disrupted the entire tax-efficient extraction strategy.
