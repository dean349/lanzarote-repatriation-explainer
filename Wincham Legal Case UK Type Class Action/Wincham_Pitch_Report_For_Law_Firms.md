## PART 3: THE WINCHAM SCHEME — WHAT HAPPENED AND WHY IT IS NEGLIGENT

### 3.1 The Scheme (Plain English)

From the mid-1990s, Wincham sold a product to British nationals who owned, or wished to own, residential property in Spain. The sales proposition was:

> *"By placing your Spanish property inside a UK Limited Company, rather than holding it in your own name, your children will inherit UK shares — not a Spanish asset. This avoids Spanish Inheritance Tax, which for non-residents can reach 81%."*

Wincham then charged each client an annual retainer (typically ~£840/year) to act as company secretary, registered office, and accountant for the UK Limited Company. Inside each company sat one Spanish residential property — typically a holiday villa or retirement home. Based on current Spanish and Canary Islands market data, the average property held by British retired couples in this scheme is valued at **€495,000 (≈£421,300)** at today's market values, though historic purchase prices were lower. The operative quantum figure is today's value, since Head 2 loss arises at the point of sale at current market prices.

Thousands of British families, predominantly retirees with no legal or tax expertise, trusted Wincham as the specialist and paid fees continuously for years or decades.

### 3.2 The Three-Layered Failure

#### Failure 1 — The Scheme Was Legally Challenged in Spain Before Brexit

The **Spanish Tax Authority (Agencia Tributaria)** has issued binding administrative rulings that it will **"look through"** UK corporate wrappers and treat a residential villa as directly owned by the individual for Spanish tax purposes, where the company has no genuine business activity beyond holding one villa. Independent Spanish law firms including **Lawbird (Marbella)** and prominent tax advisory firms including **Blevins Franks** have publicly warned about this risk in professional publications accessible to any competent accountant. Wincham never disclosed these warnings to their clients.

#### Failure 2 — Brexit Created a New and Serious Taxable Event (January 2021)

On 1 January 2021, following the UK's departure from the European Union:

- UK Limited Companies became **third-country (non-EU/non-EEA) entities** under Spanish law
- The preferential tax treatment available to EU-resident companies and individuals was removed
- The ability to offset Spanish Capital Gains Tax (CGT) against UK tax via **Foreign Tax Credit Relief (FTCR)** became severely restricted or entirely blocked for many clients
- Clients who sold their Spanish property post-Brexit faced a materially higher total tax burden than if they had been advised to exit the structure in 2019–2020

**Wincham alerted none of their clients.** They continued to issue invoices, file accounts, and collect fees as if nothing had changed.

#### Failure 3 — No Corporate Banking Infrastructure For Property Sales

Standard practice when a company-held property is sold requires the sale proceeds to be received into a **corporate bank account** in the name of the holding company. This is not optional; it flows directly from the legal relationship between the director and the company. Wincham and their subsidiary Adrem Accounting failed, as a systemic pattern, to:
- Establish or maintain active corporate bank accounts for client companies
- Advise clients of the requirement to direct sale proceeds to a company account
- Warn of the tax consequences (dividend tax at 39.35%, or Corporation Tax s.455 charge at 33.75%) where corporate funds are received in a director's personal account

**This failure is not a one-off.** Evidence shows this was a systemic, firm-wide operational deficiency affecting materially every client who reached disposal stage. Approximately **860 Wincham-administered companies** faced the same structural issue.
