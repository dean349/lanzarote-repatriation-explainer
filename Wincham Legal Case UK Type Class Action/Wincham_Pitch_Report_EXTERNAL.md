## Part 3 — The Negligence: Three Distinct, Actionable Heads

### Head 1 — Negligent Advice on Spanish Fiscal Law (Core Claim)

**What Wincham told clients:** Holding Spanish property through a UK limited company would avoid Spanish capital gains tax (Impuesto Sobre las Ganancias Patrimoniales) on disposal.

**The truth:** Spanish tax law applied the full Impuesto sobre Sociedades at 25% to any corporate disposal of Spanish real estate. From 2015, Spain's Modelo 210 also imposed **3% withholding on gross disposal proceeds** on non-resident entities at the point of sale, creating an immediate cash liability regardless of profit.

**The consequence:** Every client who sold their Spanish property incurred:
- A 3% AEAT withholding on gross proceeds (recoverable in part, but generating liquidity crises)
- Spanish corporate tax at 25% on net gain
- Significant professional fees to untangle the position
- None of which was disclosed at the point of engagement

### Head 2 — Systemic Failure to Establish Corporate Banking Infrastructure

**The operational failure:** Wincham established UK limited companies for clients but systematically failed to open functioning UK corporate bank accounts in the companies' names. This meant that when properties were sold, solicitors had nowhere to remit the proceeds — creating multiple categories of consequential loss:

- Proceeds were paid into personal accounts, creating HMRC Income Tax exposure on what should have been corporate receipts
- Professional costs of remediation (appointment of IPs, MVL expenses)
- Interest and penalties arising from delayed tax compliance
- In some cases, proceeds were paid to Wincham-associated entities directly — creating potential Proceeds of Crime Act exposure for clients unaware of the routing

**This is not an isolated failure.** Evidence shows this was a systemic, firm-wide operational deficiency affecting materially every client who reached disposal stage.

### Head 3 — Failure to Advise on Brexit Impact

From 23 June 2016 (the Brexit referendum), Wincham had a clear professional duty to advise clients that EU freedom-of-movement provisions — on which any thin justification for the scheme rested — were at existential risk. From 31 January 2020, this duty crystallised into an obligation to advise clients to unwind their positions urgently.

**Wincham did not do so.** Clients continued to hold Spanish property through UK companies until they chose (or were forced) to sell — by which point the tax position had materially worsened and the costs of unwinding had escalated.

### Route to Group Litigation

The CPR Part 19 Group Litigation Order mechanism is specifically designed for claims of this type. The common issues across all Wincham clients are:

- Substantially identical advice given (or not given) on the same statutory and regulatory framework
- Materially identical corporate structure (UK Ltd co. holding Spanish property)
- Same defendant entity or connected successor entities
- Same period of engagement (2003–2022)

A GLO would allow common issues to be tried once, dramatically reducing per-claim costs and accelerating recovery.

### Defendant Insurance Position

Despite the dissolution of Wincham Accountants Ltd and Adrem Accounting Ltd, professional indemnity insurance policies are not extinguished by dissolution. The SRA's minimum terms require run-off cover of at least 6 years post-cessation. Claims may therefore be pursued against the **historic professional indemnity insurers of Wincham / Adrem** directly or via the dissolved entity's estate.
