## SECTION 3 — ALLEGATIONS OF NEGLIGENCE

The Claimant alleges that each of the Defendants owed him a **duty of care** in the following respects, being consistent with the principles established in *Caparo Industries plc v Dickman* [1990] 2 AC 605 and *Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller & Partners Ltd* [1964] AC 465:

1. A duty to provide accurate and complete advice regarding the Spanish and UK tax consequences of the corporate wrapper scheme at the point of inception.
2. A continuing duty to monitor and advise the Claimant of any changes in law — whether in Spain, the UK, or arising from EU law — that materially affected the scheme's efficacy and the Claimant's tax position.
3. A duty to advise the Claimant, with sufficient time to act, of the consequences of Brexit for the corporate wrapper scheme, specifically prior to **31 December 2020**.
4. A duty to cease charging annual fees for services that the Defendants were no longer lawfully able to perform or that no longer provided any commercial benefit to the Claimant.

The Defendants breached each of these duties in the following specific respects:

### Negligence Ground 1 — Failure to Advise on Spanish IHT Law Changes

The Defendants represented that the corporate wrapper would protect the Claimant and his heirs from Spanish Inheritance Tax. Following the ECJ ruling in *Case C-127/12* (Commission v Spain, 2014) and the subsequent equalisation of Spanish IHT bonifications for EU/EEA nationals, the primary IHT protection rationale for the scheme was materially diminished. The Defendants failed to advise the Claimant of this change.

### Negligence Ground 2 — Failure to Advise on Spanish AEAT Anti-Avoidance Risk

The Defendants represented that selling the shares of Los Romeros Limited (rather than the underlying Spanish property) would avoid Spanish ITP. Following strengthened enforcement of Article 15, Ley 58/2003 (General Tax Law — *conflicto en la aplicación de la norma*), the AEAT could and did look through corporate wrapper structures and impose ITP on the underlying asset transfer. The Defendants failed to advise the Claimant that this protection had become legally uncertain.

### Negligence Ground 3 — Failure to Advise on Brexit Consequences (Primary Ground)

This is the primary ground of claim. The Defendants failed, at any point between 23 June 2016 and 31 December 2020, to advise the Claimant of the following specific consequences of Brexit:

**(a) Spanish IHT Regional Bonifications**
UK nationals would lose entitlement to EU/EEA-equivalent regional IHT bonifications. The Claimant's heirs would face Spanish Inheritance Tax at the higher national scale applicable to third-country nationals. The corporate wrapper — to the extent it directed Spanish succession law — now created a double exposure: full UK IHT at 40% on the UK shares AND potential Spanish IHT on the underlying Spanish asset as a third-country holding.

**(b) Capital Gains Tax Rate Uplift**
Spanish CGT rates for third-country non-residents increased post-Brexit. Had the Claimant unwound the corporate structure before 31 December 2020, the disposal would have attracted the lower EU-resident CGT rate. The failure to advise caused a quantifiable uplift in CGT liability.

**(c) Rendered Fiscal Representation Unlawful**
From 1 January 2021, the Defendants — being a UK-incorporated entity — became legally ineligible to serve as the Claimant's fiscal representative in Spain under IRNR Article 10 (Real Decreto Legislativo 5/2004). The Defendants did not disclose this to the Claimant, did not engage a qualified Spanish-resident party to take over fiscal representation, and continued to charge fees for this service.

**(d) Modelo 210/211 Filings Potentially Voidable**
All Spanish income tax and property tax returns filed by the Defendants on the Claimant's behalf after 1 January 2021 were filed by an entity with no legal standing to do so under Spanish law. These filings may be voidable by AEAT, exposing the Claimant to back-tax, penalties, and accrued interest.

**(e) Spanish Banking Access**
The Defendants failed to warn the Claimant that Spanish banks would reclassify UK companies as third-country entities post-Brexit, resulting in the loss of banking facilities, inability to receive rental income, and stranded property proceeds. This was a foreseeable and well-publicised consequence of Brexit readily available in professional tax literature no later than mid-2020.

### Negligence Ground 4 — Continued Charging of Fees for Services No Longer Deliverable

From 1 January 2021, the Defendants continued to charge the Claimant annual administration and fiscal representation fees for a service they were legally prohibited from providing. This constitutes a breach of the implied term under the **Supply of Goods and Services Act 1982, Section 13** (implied term to carry out services with reasonable care and skill) and, potentially, unjust enrichment.

### Negligence Ground 5 — Misrepresentation of Professional Status

The Defendants held themselves out as:
- "Chartered Accountants" — Wincham Accountants Limited is **not registered as a firm with the ICAEW**. Only one individual within the wider group (Leonard Jones via Adrem Accounting Ltd) holds ICAEW membership.
- FCA-regulated — In a 2014 public forum, Mark Damion Roach (Director) cited FCA Registration Number **615817**, which does **not exist** on the FCA Register. This is a potential criminal offence under Section 24, Financial Services and Markets Act 2000.
- Expert cross-border tax advisers competent to advise on Spanish tax law — in respect of which no member of the Wincham group held a Spanish professional qualification (*gestor administrativo colegiado, economista colegiado, or abogado colegiado*).

The Claimant relied on these representations in engaging and retaining the Defendants.

## SECTION 4 — CAUSATION

But for the Defendants' breaches of duty set out above, the Claimant would have:

1. Instructed an MVL of Los Romeros Limited before **31 December 2020**, at which point:
   - The property disposal would have attracted the lower EU-resident Spanish CGT rate
   - UK CGT liability could have been crystallised at that time's rates and with available reliefs
   - The structure would have been wound down in an orderly fashion under a permitted legal framework
   - Ongoing annual fees would have ceased from that date

2. Avoided the post-Brexit locked **Foreign Tax Credit Relief (FTCR)** position, whereby Spanish CGT paid cannot be fully credited against the UK CGT liability due to the differential rate uplift caused by the Defendants' delay — estimated loss: **approximately £16,800**

3. Avoided the cost of a mandatory MVL to extract the property — estimated: **£5,000–£15,000** — which would not have been required on a pre-2021 orderly wind-down

## SECTION 6 — RELEVANT LEGAL AUTHORITIES

The Claimant's claim is grounded in the following established legal principles:

| Authority | Proposition |
|---|---|
| *Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller & Partners Ltd* [1964] AC 465 | Duty of care arises from negligent misstatement by a professional held out as possessing specialist knowledge |
| *Caparo Industries plc v Dickman* [1990] 2 AC 605 | Three-stage test for duty of care: (1) foreseeability; (2) proximity; (3) fair, just, and reasonable |
| *Bolam v Friern Hospital Management Committee* [1957] 1 WLR 582 | Standard of care is that of a reasonably competent professional in the relevant field |
| *Bolitho v City and Hackney Health Authority* [1998] AC 232 | The professional's body of opinion must withstand logical analysis — Wincham's silence on Brexit cannot |
| *Vilintone & ors v Wincham International* *(settled, no published judgment)* | Crown Office Chambers record confirms this exact scheme was litigated and Wincham's insurers settled without trial — establishing real-world proof of liability |
| *Bushwood v Wincham International* *(settled, no published judgment)* | Second confirmed claim against Wincham International arising from the same scheme — same result |
| **Limitation Act 1980, s.14A** | Date of Knowledge — 3 years from the date the Claimant had or ought reasonably to have had the knowledge necessary to bring this action. The Claimant asserts that this date runs from [DATE OF DISCOVERY], not from the date of the negligent advice. |
| **Supply of Goods and Services Act 1982, s.13** | Implied term: service to be performed with reasonable care and skill |
| **Companies Act 2006, ss.172–174** | Mark Damion Roach's duties as director of Los Romeros Limited — duty to promote success; duty to exercise reasonable care, skill and diligence |
