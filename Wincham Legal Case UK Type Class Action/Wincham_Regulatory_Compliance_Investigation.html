Purpose of This Document

This report sets out the regulatory licences, professional body registrations, and government authority notifications that Wincham International Limited, Wincham Accountants Limited, Wincham Investments Limited, and associated entities were legally required to hold in order to operate the Wincham scheme lawfully. It identifies, jurisdiction by jurisdiction, where Wincham appears to have operated without the required authorisation, registration, or regulatory oversight — and why each gap is legally significant for any professional negligence or regulatory complaint proceeding.

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The Wincham scheme — selling UK limited company wrappers to British nationals holding Spanish residential property, combined with ongoing company secretarial, accountancy, and tax advisory services — crossed at least seven distinct regulatory thresholds across two jurisdictions. To operate lawfully, Wincham required:

In the United Kingdom: 1. HMRC registration as a Trust or Company Service Provider (TCSP) under the Money Laundering Regulations 2017 2. Membership of a recognised professional accountancy body (ICAEW, ACCA, or equivalent) with valid AML supervisory status — or direct HMRC supervision 3. Compliance with the Companies Act 2006 duties applicable to nominee directors holding mass simultaneous appointments 4. If providing financial or tax-planning advice crossing into investment territory: FCA authorisation under FSMA 2000, or exemption via a Designated Professional Body (DPB) 5. Compliance with Section 21 FSMA 2000 in relation to financial promotions for investment structures 6. Registration under the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 obligations (professional diligence standard) 7. HMRC mandatory tax agent registration (from May 2026 — but existing duty of competence applied throughout)

In Spain / Canary Islands (Lanzarote): 1. Registration with Spain's Agencia Tributaria (AEAT) as a fiscal representative (representante fiscal) for non-resident companies 2. Registration of any Spanish business presence with the Registro Mercantil (Spanish Commercial Registry) and obtaining a CIF/NIF (Spanish company tax number) 3. Membership of a Spanish professional body — specifically the Colegio de Economistas or Colegio de Gestores Administrativos — for any person acting in a regulatory capacity before the AEAT 4. Entering into a Convenio de Colaboración Social with the AEAT to file tax returns on behalf of third parties electronically 5. Appointment of a residente fiscal español (resident fiscal representative) for clients with Spanish property after Brexit 6. Compliance with the Canary Islands special tax regime (ZEC / IGIC) for any commercial activity operating within the Canary Islands fiscal territory

PART 1: UNITED KINGDOM — REQUIRED LICENCES AND REGISTRATIONS

1.1 Trust or Company Service Provider (TCSP) Registration

Regulatory basis:
Money Laundering, Terrorist Financing and Transfer of Funds (Information on the Payer) Regulations 2017 (MLR 2017), Regulation 8 and Schedule 1.

What Wincham did that required this registration:

Every single service Wincham provided to its circa 589 UK limited company clients falls squarely within the statutory definition of a Trust or Company Service Provider (TCSP):

Wincham Activity TCSP Category Under MLR 2017
Forming UK limited companies for clients "Forming companies or other legal persons"
Providing registered office address (Wincham House) for client companies "Providing a registered office, business address, or correspondence address"
Acting as company secretary for each client company "Acting as, or arranging for another person to act as, secretary of a company"
Providing nominee directors (Jon Antony Hallatt et al.) "Acting as, or arranging for another person to act as, director of a company"
Providing nominee shareholders "Acting as, or arranging for another person to act as, a nominee shareholder"

Critically: The MLR 2017 explicitly states that a firm is a TCSP even if these services are provided occasionally or as an incidental part of broader accountancy services. Wincham provided these services as the core of its business model, at industrial scale (~589 simultaneous client companies).

What was required:

Wincham was required either to: - Be supervised by a recognised professional body AML supervisor (e.g., ICAEW, ACCA, AAT), which would automatically register it as a TCSP with HMRC; or - Register directly with HMRC as a TCSP under the MLR 2017, passing the "fit and proper" test for all beneficial owners, officers, and managers

The gap:
There is no public record of Wincham Accountants Limited, Wincham International Limited, or Adrem Accounting Limited being registered as a TCSP with HMRC. Wincham's FCA registration (FRN 615817) was false, and their professional body membership is unconfirmed. If Wincham was not supervised by a professional body for AML purposes, operating as a TCSP without HMRC registration is a criminal offence under the MLR 2017.

⚠️ WARNING

Criminal exposure: Operating a TCSP business without registration is punishable by an unlimited fine and/or up to two years imprisonment (MLR 2017, Regulation 86). This is distinct from civil professional negligence and represents a potentially standalone criminal compliance failure.

1.2 Professional Body Membership — ICAEW, ACCA, or Equivalent

Regulatory basis:
- Companies Act 2006, ss. 172, 174 - Supply of Goods and Services Act 1982, s. 13 - Professional standards of the Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales (ICAEW); Association of Chartered Certified Accountants (ACCA); Chartered Institute of Taxation (CIOT)

What Wincham presented itself as:

Wincham described itself publicly as a "chartered accountancy" firm providing specialist tax and accountancy services for clients with overseas property. This representation implies membership of a recognised professional body. Membership of ICAEW, ACCA, or an equivalent body:

  • Requires passing professional examinations
  • Requires maintaining Continuing Professional Development (CPD) in relevant fields
  • Requires carrying Professional Indemnity Insurance meeting minimum standards
  • Subjects the member to disciplinary oversight and a code of professional ethics
  • For cross-border tax work (UK-Spanish dual jurisdiction), requires demonstrating competency in both jurisdictions' tax regimes

The gap:
No confirmed evidence exists of Wincham Accountants Limited or Adrem Accounting Limited holding current ICAEW, ACCA, or CIOT membership at the relevant times. The ICAEW member search at find.icaew.com should be checked against all key individuals (Malcolm David Roach, Mark Damion Roach).

If Wincham held no professional body membership throughout the period of scheme operation, it was: - Holding itself out as a "chartered accountancy" firm without the right to do so — a misrepresentation actionable under the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations 2008 (CPRs 2008) - Operating without AML supervision, which is a criminal offence under the MLR 2017 - Providing accountancy services without the standard of professional competence users had a right to expect

1.3 FCA Authorisation — Financial Services and Markets Act 2000

Regulatory basis:
Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA 2000), ss. 19, 21, 23

What Wincham did that could require FCA authorisation:

FSMA 2000, s.19 makes it a criminal offence for any person to carry out a "regulated activity" in the UK unless authorised by the FCA or exempt. The Regulated Activities Order 2001 (RAO) specifies which activities are regulated.

Wincham's scheme potentially crosses into regulated territory in the following ways:

Wincham Activity Relevant Regulated Activity Under RAO
Advising clients to restructure their assets (place property into a UK Ltd) specifically to achieve a tax outcome Article 53 RAO — Advising on investments (where the structure itself is an "investment")
Selling the concept of the UK corporate wrapper as a financial planning tool to avoid inheritance tax Article 25 RAO — Arranging deals in investments
Acting as nominee director/shareholder for client corporate structures Article 40 RAO — Safeguarding and administering investments
Managing the corporate affairs of ~589 investment holding vehicles simultaneously Article 37 RAO — Managing investments

The Section 21 FSMA financial promotion issue:

Section 21 FSMA prohibits the communication of any "invitation or inducement to engage in investment activity" unless the communicator is FCA-authorised, or the communication is approved by an FCA-authorised person. Wincham's marketing materials promoting the Spanish property UK corporate scheme — on its website, in printed materials, and via agents — may constitute a financial promotion if the scheme crossed the line from pure accountancy advice into structured investment product territory.

A breach of s.21 FSMA is: - A criminal offence carrying up to two years imprisonment and/or an unlimited fine - Renders any resulting contract unenforceable against the client, and entitles the client to recover money paid and compensation for losses

The FCA false registration claim:

Most critically, in 2014, Mark Roach publicly claimed FCA registration number 615817. This number has been confirmed as non-existent on the FCA Financial Services Register.

Claiming to be FCA-registered when not authorised is: - A criminal offence under s.24 FSMA 2000 (making false claims of authorisation) - A deceptive practice actionable under the CPRs 2008 - A material misrepresentation that induced clients (including Philip Harrison's predecessor in Los Romeros Limited) to place trust in Wincham's regulatory credentials

What actual FCA authorisation existed:

Belgrave Wincham Limited (FRN 912897) — incorporated August 2019 — is listed on the FCA Register only as an Appointed Representative of another firm (not as a directly-authorised firm). It was incorporated 5 years after the false FRN 615817 claim. Its directors are Craig Owen Roberts and Thomas Adam Foulkes — different individuals from Mark Roach — suggesting it was constituted as a separate compliance vehicle rather than a continuation of the Wincham scheme.

1.4 HMRC Anti-Money Laundering Supervision (Direct)

Regulatory basis:
MLR 2017, Part 2; HMRC TCSP Register

Requirement:
All businesses providing TCSP services (see §1.1 above) that are not supervised for AML purposes by a professional body (ICAEW, ACCA, SRA, etc.) must register directly with HMRC for AML supervision and pass the "fit and proper" test. This is not optional; it is a legal obligation.

The gap:
If Wincham held no professional body membership (see §1.2), it was required to register directly with HMRC. The HMRC TCSP register is publicly searchable at www.gov.uk/anti-money-laundering-registration. An investigative search of this register for all Wincham entities is required as a recommended next step.

Given Mark Roach's 431 simultaneous company directorships, it is extremely doubtful that the required "fit and proper" assessment ever took place. The "fit and proper" test specifically examines whether a business is capable of providing genuine fiduciary oversight to its clients — a test that 431 simultaneous appointments inherently fails.

1.6 Companies Act 2006 — Nominee Director Duties

Regulatory basis:
Companies Act 2006, ss. 170–177 (Directors' Duties)

The issue:
Wincham's business model involved providing nominee directors (primarily Jon Antony Hallatt, who held 180+ simultaneous appointments at the same address) to act as formal directors of client companies. Under the Companies Act 2006, a company director — whether nominee or not — owes the company the following statutory duties:

  • s.172 — Duty to act in the way most likely to promote the success of the company
  • s.174 — Duty to exercise reasonable care, skill, and diligence (including the care of a reasonably diligent person with the general knowledge possessed by a director)
  • s.175 — Duty to avoid conflicts of interest

The gap:
A nominee director who simultaneously holds 180+ directorial appointments at the same address, across 180+ separate companies with 180+ separate beneficial owners, cannot — by any reasonable construction — be exercising the s.174 duty of reasonable care for each individual company. The nominee director arrangement was, by its structural nature, in systemic breach of the Companies Act 2006 duties Wincham represented to clients it was fulfilling.

This is directly relevant to the professional negligence claim: the nominee director services Wincham charged annual fees for were inherently incapable of meeting the statutory standard those fees were supposed to procure.

1.7 HMRC Mandatory Tax Agent Registration (From May 2026)

Regulatory basis:
Finance Act 2022, Schedule 2 (HMRC registration of tax advisers); HMRC Consultation Response (2024)

Note: The mandatory HMRC registration regime for tax advisers who interact with HMRC on behalf of clients comes into force in May 2026. However, the underlying duty of professional competence — to maintain knowledge of tax law developments relevant to clients — existed throughout Wincham's operation. The new registration regime simply formalises a standard that a competent tax adviser was already required to meet.

  • Telephone: 0034 965 830 991
  • Wincham's website confirms the Calpe office as its Spanish base for providing "fiscal representation, tax declarations, CIF number applications, and Spanish tax advice"

Note on Lanzarote specifically:
Lanzarote is part of the Canary Islands (Islas Canarias), which is an autonomous community of Spain within the province of Las Palmas. The Canary Islands have a special fiscal status within the EU/Spain, operating under the IGIC (Impuesto General Indirecto Canario) rather than mainland Spanish IVA (VAT). Wincham had no confirmed registered office or registered subsidiary in Lanzarote. However, it was providing services to clients with Lanzarote property (including Philip Harrison's villa), which creates its own regulatory obligations relative to the Canary Islands tax authority.

2.2 Agencia Tributaria (AEAT) — Fiscal Representative Registration

Regulatory basis:
- Ley 35/2006 (IRPF — Personal Income Tax Law) - Real Decreto 1776/2004 (Non-Resident Income Tax Regulations) - Ley 58/2003 General Tributaria (General Tax Law) - Article 10, Real Decreto 1065/2007 (Regulations for tax management and inspection)

What was required:

Wincham was acting as fiscal representative (representante fiscal) before the Agencia Tributaria for non-resident UK companies and their directors who owned Spanish property. This role:

  1. Requires formal appointment via notarised poder notarial (notarised power of attorney) from each client company/director
  2. Requires the representative to be resident in Spain or, post-Brexit, in the EU/EEA for the representation to be valid (post-2021, UK companies became third-country entities)
  3. Requires filing Modelo 036 with the AEAT to declare the commencement of representation
  4. Creates joint and several liability for the AEAT obligations of the represented entity — meaning Wincham's Spanish office was, in theory, co-liable for client companies' Spanish tax obligations

The post-Brexit gap:
After 1 January 2021, UK residents and UK companies became non-EU entities for Spanish tax purposes. The rules on fiscal representation for non-EU entities specifically require the fiscal representative to be resident in the EU (or at minimum, in Spain). Wincham, as a UK entity, lost its ability to act as fiscal representative for UK non-resident individuals after Brexit — yet continued to do so, collecting fees without apparently notifying clients of the change in their legal position.

2.3 Registro Mercantil — Spanish Commercial Registry Registration

Regulatory basis:
Código de Comercio (Commercial Code) and Real Decreto 1784/1996 (Reglamento del Registro Mercantil)

What was required:

If Wincham's Spanish office in Calpe constituted a "permanent establishment" (establecimiento permanente) of a foreign company in Spain — which it almost certainly did, given it had a physical address, telephone number, staff, and was conducting regular business activity in Spain — Wincham was required to:

  1. Register the branch or permanent establishment with the Registro Mercantil (local Commercial Registry — in Alicante's case, the Registro Mercantil de Alicante)
  2. Obtain a CIF (Código de Identificación Fiscal) from the AEAT for the Spanish establishment
  3. File annual accounts in Spain in relation to the permanent establishment's activities
  4. Pay Spanish Corporation Tax (IS — Impuesto sobre Sociedades) on income attributable to the Spanish permanent establishment

If Wincham's Calpe office was not formally registered as a permanent establishment, it was operating in breach of Spanish commercial law, which requires all foreign companies with a habitual commercial presence in Spain to register with the Registro Mercantil.

2.4 Colegio de Economistas / Professional Body Membership — Spain

Regulatory basis:
- Ley 2/1974 sobre Colegios Profesionales (Professional Associations Law, as amended) - Estatutos del Consejo General de Colegios de Economistas de España - Estatutos del Consejo General de Colegios de Gestores Administrativos de España

What was required:

In Spain, the two main regulated professional categories that interact with the AEAT on behalf of third parties are:

Professional Category Body Requirement
Gestor Administrativo Colegiado Consejo General de Colegios de Gestores Administrativos University degree + professional examination + registration with official Colegio
Economista Colegiado Consejo General de Colegios de Economistas Economics/Business degree + registration with official Colegio
Asesor Fiscal (via AEDAF or REAF-REGAF) Registro de Economistas Asesores Fiscales (REAF) Membership of REAF within Consejo General de Economistas
Abogado (Lawyer) Consejo General de la Abogacía Española / Local Bar Law degree + professional practice exam + registration with local Colegio de Abogados

The gap:
Wincham's website claimed to employ "Gestores, Economistas, and Abogados" at its Spanish office. For those claims to be accurate and lawful, the individuals named must be colegiados (members of their respective official professional bodies). An individual acting before the AEAT as a gestor without being a Gestor Administrativo Colegiado is committing an infraction of Spanish professional law. If Wincham's Spanish staff were not properly colegiados, every tax filing, fiscal representation, and AEAT interaction they conducted on behalf of clients was of questionable legitimacy.

Recommended verification: The membership of any named Wincham Spain staff member can be verified through the respective provincial Colegio (Colegio de Economistas de Alicante; Colegio de Gestores Administrativos de Alicante).

2.5 Convenio de Colaboración Social — AEAT Filing Agreement

Regulatory basis:
Article 92, Ley 58/2003 (General Tax Law); AEAT Resolution of 26 December 2007

What was required:

To file tax returns electronically on behalf of third parties through the AEAT's online portal, professional firms must enter into a Convenio de Colaboración Social (Social Collaboration Agreement) with the AEAT. This agreement:

  • Grants the firm the right to file tax returns electronically on behalf of clients
  • Requires the firm to accept AEAT's terms of use, data security requirements, and compliance obligations
  • Is revocable by the AEAT if the firm fails to comply with filing standards

Without this agreement, Wincham had no legal authority to file Modelo 210 (non-resident income tax), Modelo 211 (Spanish CGT retention), or any other AEAT filing on behalf of client companies.

The gap:
If Wincham was filing AEAT returns for ~589 client companies without a valid Convenio de Colaboración Social, every such filing was of uncertain legal standing. If errors were made or returns were filed incorrectly, clients would have no recourse against the AEAT for inadequate representation by an unauthorised agent.

2.6 Canary Islands Specific Obligations — IGIC and ZEC

Regulatory basis:
Ley 20/1991 de 7 de junio (Canary Islands Tax Regime); Ley 19/1994 de 6 de julio (Economic and Tax Regime of the Canary Islands — ZEC)

Canary Islands special fiscal status:

The Canary Islands — including Lanzarote — are a special EU outermost region and are not part of the EU Customs Union or EU VAT area. They operate under their own indirect tax: IGIC (Impuesto General Indirecto Canario) rather than mainland Spanish IVA. This means:

  1. Any company providing services commercially in Lanzarote must consider IGIC registration and compliance (not mainland IVA/VAT)
  2. Any fiscal representative operating on behalf of property-owning entities in the Canary Islands must be familiar with Canary Islands-specific tax regulations, which differ materially from mainland Spanish law
  3. UK company structures holding Lanzarote property face different Spanish IHT look-through rules in the Canary Islands, governed by the Decreto Legislativo 1/2009 (Canary Islands IHT Law)

The gap:
Wincham's Calpe (mainland) office was advising on Canary Islands property matters without any confirmed presence, expertise, or regulatory registration in the Canary Islands (Las Palmas province). The specific IGIC and Canary Islands tax obligations applicable to UK nationals holding Lanzarote property through UK corporate structures require specialist Canary Islands-qualified expertise — which Wincham does not appear to have had.

PART 4: LEGAL SIGNIFICANCE FOR THE CLAIM

4.1 Why the Regulatory Gaps Strengthen the Negligence Case

Professional negligence claims against Wincham do not require proving regulatory breaches — they require proving Wincham fell below the standard of a competent accountant. However, the regulatory gaps documented here are extraordinarily useful for three reasons:

1. They corroborate incompetence. A firm that cannot or does not obtain the regulatory registrations required for its own business operations cannot credibly argue it provided competent cross-border tax advice to its clients.

2. They provide additional heads of claim. The false FCA claim (FRN 615817) and any CPRs 2008 violations are standalone causes of action that can be pursued independently of the professional negligence claim, potentially with different limitation periods and remedies.

3. They undermine any competence defence. Wincham's most likely defence is "we provided a legitimate service that was overtaken by regulatory change." The discovery that Wincham was simultaneously operating without mandatory TCSP registration, without confirmed professional body membership, and with a false FCA registration claim, demolishes any narrative of a competent, compliant professional firm that simply encountered an unexpected legal development.

4.2 The False FCA Claim as a Standalone Claim

Under s.24 FSMA 2000, it is a criminal offence to: - Describe oneself as authorised by the FCA when not - Describe oneself as exempt from the general prohibition when not exempt - Behave in a manner indicating authorisation or exemption when not

Mark Roach's 2014 public forum claim of FCA FRN 615817 (non-existent) may constitute a s.24 FSMA offence. This is a matter for the FCA's enforcement division and is a potential referral point once Philip Harrison's individual claim is underway.

4.3 The Post-Brexit Spanish Representation Gap

This is possibly the most devastating and underappreciated regulatory failure in the entire scheme. After 1 January 2021: - UK entities became third-country non-EU entities under Spanish law - Spanish law requires non-EU fiscal representatives to be resident in Spain or the EU - Wincham (a UK entity) lost its legal eligibility to act as fiscal representative for its UK non-resident clients under the very Spanish law framework it was supposed to be administering

In other words: the firm Wincham clients were paying to handle their Spanish tax obligations after Brexit may have been legally incapable of fulfilling that role under Spanish law from 1 January 2021 onwards. Every Modelo 210, Modelo 211, and AEAT filing submitted by Wincham on behalf of a UK non-resident client since that date is of questionable validity.
