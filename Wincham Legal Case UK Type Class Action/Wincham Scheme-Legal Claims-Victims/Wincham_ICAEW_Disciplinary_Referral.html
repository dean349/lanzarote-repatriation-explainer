PART 2 — NATURE OF THE COMPLAINT

This complaint alleges that Leonard Edward Jones, as a member of the ICAEW, has committed multiple serious and concurrent breaches of the ICAEW Code of Ethics (based on the IESBA International Ethics Standards Board for Accountants Code), including:

  1. Fundamental breach of the Independence principle (Code of Ethics Section 120) — by secretly acquiring beneficial ownership (75%+) of a client-facing entity while simultaneously acting as its auditor/accountant and presenting to clients as an independent professional.

  2. Breach of the Objectivity principle (Code of Ethics Section 120) — by allowing the financial interest described above to compromise, or be seen to compromise, his professional judgement.

  3. Breach of the Integrity principle (Code of Ethics Section 111)** — by operating under the brand name "Wincham" and allowing clients to believe they were contracting with an ICAEW-regulated firm, when in fact the Wincham-branded entities held no ICAEW registration.

  4. Failure to comply with NOCLAR obligations (Code of Ethics Section 360) — Non-Compliance with Laws and Regulations — specifically by failing to alert regulatory authorities to the following criminal and regulatory breaches within the group he controls: unlicensed TCSP activity (MLR 2017 Regulation 86), unlicensed financial intermediation (FSMA 2000), and a 20-month delayed PSC filing (Companies Act 2006, s.790W).

  5. Bringing the ICAEW into disrepute — by using the ICAEW's regulatory standing as a marketing shield for an unregistered corporate group operating outside the ICAEW's regulatory perimeter, thereby exploiting client reliance on the ICAEW brand without entitlement.

PART 5 — SPECIFIC ICAEW CODE OF ETHICS BREACHES

Breach 1 — Independence (Section 120 and Part 4A)

The ICAEW Code of Ethics, drawing on the IESBA Code, requires that a member in public practice maintain independence — in mind and in appearance — from audit clients and from entities in respect of which the member provides assurance or professional services.

Where a member acquires a financial interest (including beneficial ownership) in an entity for which they provide professional services, this creates a threat to independence that cannot be managed by safeguards alone — it requires the member to either divest the interest or cease providing professional services.

Leonard Jones acquired 75%+ beneficial ownership of Agere International Ltd (the Wincham trading entity) in August 2023. From that date: - He or his firm (Adrem Accounting Ltd) continued to provide accountancy and secretarial services to Wincham group entities; - He continued to present to clients as an independent ICAEW professional; and - His ICAEW registration continued to be used — via his @wincham.com email address on the ICAEW public register — to create the impression of professional independence.

This is a fundamental and irremediable threat to independence. No safeguard is capable of addressing a situation where the accountant is also the 75%+ owner of the entity for which they are providing professional services.

Breach 2 — Integrity (Section 111)

Section 111 of the Code requires members to be straightforward and honest in all professional and business relationships. A member must not be associated with any information that they know to be false or misleading.

Leonard Jones allowed, and continues to allow, the ICAEW public register to present him as a member associated with a "@wincham.com" email — thereby implicitly representing to clients and third parties that "Wincham" is an ICAEW-regulated firm. This representation is materially false: no Wincham-branded entity holds ICAEW registration. This deliberate co-mingling of his ICAEW credentials with an unregistered trading group constitutes a material misrepresentation.

Breach 3 — Objectivity (Section 120)

An ICAEW member must not allow bias, conflict of interest, or undue influence to override professional judgements. A 75%+ ownership stake in an entity creates an obvious financial self-interest that is irreconcilable with objective professional advice to clients of that entity.

Breach 4 — Professional Competence and Due Care (Section 113)

A member acting as company secretary of an entity that fails to file a PSC change for 20 months — when the 14-day filing deadline is a clear statutory obligation — has failed to perform their duties with professional competence and due care.

Breach 5 — Professional Behaviour (Section 115) — Bringing the Profession into Disrepute

Using ICAEW membership as a marketing credibility device for an unregistered advisory group — particularly one that has now been found to be unregistered with HMRC, unlicensed by the FCA, and operating a financial intermediation business without authorisation — brings the ICAEW and the accountancy profession into disrepute.

Breach 6 — Professional Competence and Due Care (Section 113) — Systematic SIC Code Mis-classification (Full-Scheme: 399 Companies, Confirmed 2 April 2026)

Forensic analysis of Companies House records, confirmed by live API audit on 2 April 2026, reveals that 399 of 752 companies administered across both Wincham registered addresses (53.1%) are filed under SIC code 70229 (Management Consultancy activities other than financial management) — a classification entirely inconsistent with the companies' actual purpose as UK corporate vehicles for Spanish property ownership.

The cohort breakdown is: 167 of 197 Adrem (CW12 4AA) companies (85%) and 232 of 585 Wincham (CW12 4TR) companies (39.7%).

The correct SIC codes for these entities are 68100 (Buying and selling of own real estate) or 68209 (Other letting and operating of own or leased real estate).

An ICAEW-registered accountant acting as a filing agent or company secretary for client companies bears professional responsibility for the accuracy of annual Confirmation Statements, which include the SIC code declaration. The systematic application of a single, generic, and demonstrably incorrect SIC code across 399 administered companies:

  • Constitutes a failure of professional competence and due care under Section 113 of the ICAEW Code of Ethics;
  • Creates a material risk of ATED (Annual Tax on Enveloped Dwellings) compliance failures for affected companies, given that HMRC's automated profiling systems use SIC codes as a classification indicator;
  • Is forensic evidence that client companies were being administered as an industrial batch across a 17-year scheme rather than as individual professional client engagements; and
  • May constitute an offence under section 853E of the Companies Act 2006 (filing incorrect information on a Confirmation Statement) for each of the 399 affected filings.

The client company most directly relevant to this complaint — Los Romeros Limited (Co. 06993349) — is confirmed as one of the 399 mis-classified entities, with SIC 70229 filed despite it being a passive Spanish property holding vehicle.
