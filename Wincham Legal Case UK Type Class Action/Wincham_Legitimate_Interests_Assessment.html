Purpose of this document: This Legitimate Interests Assessment has been prepared in accordance with the requirements of the UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) and the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) guidance on legitimate interests (published at ico.org.uk). It documents the three-part test applied before relying on Article 6(1)(f) as the lawful basis for assembling, holding, and disclosing the Wincham Victim Database (defined below) to regulated UK legal services providers.

PART 1 — BACKGROUND AND CONTEXT

1.1 Who Is the Data Controller?

Dean Harrison (hereinafter "the Controller") is an individual based in the United States of America. The Controller assembled the Wincham Victim Database (defined at 1.2 below) through a systematic forensic cross-referencing process using publicly accessible UK government registers, conducted between January 2026 and April 2026.

The Controller operates as a commercial data licensor. The Controller does not conduct regulated legal, financial, or claims management activity in the United Kingdom.

1.2 What Is the Wincham Victim Database?

The "Wincham Victim Database" is a structured dataset comprising approximately 1,564 records across 782 UK limited companies that were incorporated and/or administered by:

  • Wincham Accountants Limited (Companies House No. 05607266) and associated Wincham Group entities, registered at Wincham House, Greenfield Farm Trading Estate, Congleton, Cheshire, CW12 4TR; and
  • Adrem Accounting Ltd (Companies House No. 05984803), registered at Albert Chambers, Congleton, Cheshire, CW12 4AA.

Each record contains the following data fields:

Field Source
Company name UK Companies House public register
Companies House number UK Companies House public register
Company status (active / dissolved / struck off) UK Companies House public register
Date of incorporation UK Companies House public register
Director full name UK Companies House public register
Director registered address UK Companies House public register

All data was sourced exclusively from the UK Companies House public register, which is a statutory public register maintained by Companies House under the Companies Act 2006. Access to this register is free and open to any member of the public under UK law.

1.3 What Processing Activity Does This LIA Cover?

This LIA covers the following processing activities:

  1. Assembly — collecting and structuring the above data fields from the Companies House public register into a single database
  2. Storage — retaining the database in electronic form (CSV format) on the Controller's private systems
  3. Disclosure — transferring the database (or a portion of it) to a regulated UK law firm or claims management company (the "Licensee") pursuant to a formal Data Licence Agreement, for the purpose described at Part 2 below
  4. Ongoing retention — retaining the database for the duration of the Data Licence Agreement

This LIA does not cover, and does not provide a lawful basis for:

  • Direct contact with any individual in the database by the Controller or any introducer
  • Use of the database for any purpose other than the Permitted Purpose defined at Part 2
  • Transfer of the database to any unregulated third party

PART 2 — THE THREE-PART TEST

STEP 1: THE PURPOSE TEST

"Does a legitimate interest exist?"

1A. What Is the Legitimate Interest?

The Controller's legitimate interest is:

To facilitate access to legal redress for UK individuals who were clients of Wincham Accountants Limited and Adrem Accounting Ltd, and who have suffered quantifiable financial loss as a result of systemic professional negligence by those firms, by assembling and making available to regulated UK legal practitioners a structured dataset that enables those practitioners to identify and contact potential claimants.

This interest operates on two levels:

Commercial interest: The Controller has invested substantial time and technical resource in assembling, cleaning, de-duplicating, and structuring the database. Licensing the database to regulated legal practitioners generates commercial income for the Controller. Commercial interests are expressly recognised as capable of constituting legitimate interests under Article 6(1)(f) UK GDPR, confirmed by the ICO and by the CJEU in Rigas satiksme (C-13/16).

Public interest / access to justice: The wider interest in enabling victims of professional wrongdoing to access legal representation is a recognised public interest. The following facts support this characterisation:

  • Wincham Accountants and Adrem Accounting operated a scheme marketed to thousands of British retirees, the majority of whom had no legal or tax expertise
  • Independent professional analysis (published by Lawbird Legal Services, Blevins Franks International, and others) has confirmed that the advice given by Wincham carried material legal and tax risks that were never disclosed to clients
  • A barrister at Crown Office Chambers (London) — Michael Harper — has already successfully litigated claims against Wincham International arising from this exact scheme (Vilintone & ors v Wincham International; Bushwood v Wincham International), confirming the legal theory is sound
  • Multiple individuals in the database are currently actively seeking to unwind their Wincham structures (evidenced by MVL notices in the London Gazette), demonstrating ongoing urgency and active need

Conclusion — Step 1: ✅ A genuine legitimate interest exists. It is both commercial and connected to the public interest in access to justice.

1B. Whose Legitimate Interests Are Relied Upon?

  • Primarily: the Controller's commercial interest in licensing the database
  • Secondarily: the Licensee law firm's interest in identifying and advising potential clients
  • Thirdly: the interests of the potential claimants themselves in being made aware, through their solicitors, that they may have actionable professional negligence claims

STEP 2: THE NECESSITY TEST

"Is the processing necessary to achieve the legitimate interest?"

2A. Could the Legitimate Interest Be Achieved Without Processing Personal Data?

No. The purpose of the database is to enable a regulated law firm to identify and contact the directors of Wincham-administered companies — the actual human individuals who were Wincham's clients and who may have suffered loss. This requires:

  • Director names — to address formal legal correspondence to the correct individual
  • Director registered addresses — to deliver that correspondence

Without these data fields, the database would contain only company-level information (company name and number), which would be insufficient to enable outreach to affected individuals.

Alternatives considered:

Alternative Why Rejected
Publishing the company list publicly without director details Would not enable the law firm to contact affected individuals
Waiting for individuals to self-identify on a landing page A supplemental and slower route — does not replace the need for the database, particularly given the urgency of limitation deadlines
Providing only company names to the law firm (no personal data) The law firm could query Companies House independently, but this would duplicate the Controller's work product and undermine the commercial data licence model

Conclusion — Step 2: ✅ The processing of personal data (director names and addresses) is necessary. There is no less privacy-intrusive method that would achieve the same result.

2B. Is the Scale of Data Proportionate?

The database contains the minimum fields necessary for the identified purpose. The Controller does not collect or hold:

  • Financial account details
  • Tax reference numbers
  • National Insurance numbers
  • Passport or ID document data
  • Health or biometric data
  • Political or religious views

The data fields held (name, registered address, company details) are the minimum required to enable a regulated legal practitioner to direct a formal legal letter to the correct individual.

STEP 3: THE BALANCING TEST

"Do the individual's interests or rights override the legitimate interest?"

This is the most important part of the assessment. The Controller has weighed the following factors:

3A. Nature of the Data

Factor Assessment
Source of data The UK Companies House public register — a statutory register that Parliament has specifically designed to be publicly accessible. Directors of UK limited companies are legally required to provide their details to Companies House and to consent to their publication.
Sensitivity of data Low to medium. Director names and registered addresses are not special category data under Article 9 UK GDPR. They are, by statute, public. However, the Controller acknowledges that when combined with the context of potential litigation, this creates a degree of sensitivity that must be weighted in the balancing test.
Reasonable expectation of directors Any individual who agrees to become the director of a UK limited company administered by an accountancy firm, and whose details are filed at Companies House, has a reasonable expectation that those details may be accessed by third parties — including legal practitioners, claimant researchers, and regulatory bodies — for purposes connected with that directorship.

3B. The Impact on the Data Subjects

Factor Assessment
Nature of the contact they will receive A formal legal letter from an SRA-regulated law firm, advising them of their potential legal rights and inviting them to explore a claim. This is a single, non-intrusive contact.
Impact if they have a valid claim Positive — they will be made aware of a right to financial compensation that they would otherwise not have known about
Impact if they do not have a valid claim Minimal — they receive one letter, which they may ignore. The law firm (as a regulated entity) is trained to triage properly and will not pursue individuals without a sound basis.
Would a reasonable person object? It is unlikely that a person who unknowingly suffered financial loss due to professional negligence would object to a law firm contacting them to advise them of their legal rights. The purpose of the contact is to benefit them, not to exploit them.
Risk of harm Low. The processing does not involve public disclosure of the individuals' details. Data is transferred only to a single regulated law firm under a contractually binding Data Licence Agreement prohibiting any further disclosure or use.

3C. Safeguards Applied

The following safeguards are in place to protect data subject interests and are reflected in the Data Licence Agreement:

Safeguard Detail
Restricted access The database is released only to SRA-regulated law firms or FCA-authorised claims management companies, under a signed Data Licence Agreement
Non-sublicensable licence The Licensee cannot pass the data on to any third party without the Controller's written consent
Permitted Purpose restriction The Licensee may use the data only to identify and contact potential claimants for professional negligence proceedings against Wincham and associated entities
No public disclosure The database is not published, broadcast, or shared beyond the single Licensee
Data Licence Agreement Contractually binds the Licensee to UK GDPR compliance, appropriate technical and organisational security measures, and prohibits use after termination
Limitation on fields Only the minimum necessary data fields are included in the licensed dataset
Right to object Any individual who contacts the Controller to object to their data being processed will have their record permanently removed from the database

3D. Balancing Conclusion

Weighing Finding
Controller's interest Real, substantial, and connected to access to justice
Data subject's likely interest Their interest in knowing about their legal rights outweighs their privacy interest in not receiving a single formal letter from a regulated legal practitioner
Harm risk Minimal — single regulated contact; data not publicly disclosed
Reasonable expectation Directors of UK companies on a public statutory register reasonably expect their details to be accessible
Overall conclusion The Controller's legitimate interests are not overridden by the data subjects' interests or fundamental rights and freedoms

Conclusion — Step 3: ✅ The balancing test is passed. Article 6(1)(f) is a valid lawful basis for this processing.

PART 3 — OVERALL CONCLUSION

Having applied the full three-part test required by Article 6(1)(f) UK GDPR and the ICO's guidance on legitimate interests, the Controller concludes:

The assembly, storage, and disclosure of the Wincham Victim Database to regulated UK legal practitioners, for the purpose of enabling contact with potential claimants in professional negligence proceedings against Wincham Accountants Limited and associated entities, is lawful under Article 6(1)(f) UK GDPR (Legitimate Interests).

This conclusion is supported by:
- The public and statutory nature of the source data (Companies House)
- The access-to-justice public interest underpinning the purpose
- The restriction of disclosure to a single SRA-regulated entity at any one time
- The contractual safeguards limiting use to the Permitted Purpose
- The low harm risk and high potential benefit to data subjects
- The reasonable expectation of directors of publicly-registered UK companies

PART 4 — ADDITIONAL COMPLIANCE NOTES

4.1 Article 14 — Transparency Notice to Data Subjects

UK GDPR Article 14 requires that where personal data is not collected directly from the data subject, the Controller must provide a privacy notice to those individuals "within a reasonable period" of first processing their data, and no later than one month after collection, OR at the point of first disclosure, whichever is earlier.

Practical position: The obligation to notify data subjects under Article 14 rests on the Licensee (the law firm) at the point it contacts individuals, as it is the Licensee that makes the initial contact. The Licensee, as an SRA-regulated firm, operates under its own GDPR compliance framework and privacy obligations.

The Controller recommends including a right-to-object and privacy notice in the Licensee's initial contact letter to each individual.

Article 14(5)(b) exception: The obligation to notify may be disapplied where providing such notice "proves impossible or would involve a disproportionate effort." Given the number of records and the absence of email addresses in the dataset, notification by the Controller independent of the Licensee's outreach would involve disproportionate effort. This exception is available but the Licensee's contact letter remains the recommended vehicle for the required notification.

4.2 Data Retention

The Controller will retain the database for the duration of active Data Licence Agreements plus a further six years (consistent with the Limitation Act 1980 primary limitation period for contract claims) to enable enforcement of the NDA and Data Licence Agreement if required.

4.3 Data Security

The database is stored in encrypted form on the Controller's private systems. The Controller will transfer the dataset to the Licensee by secure electronic means only (encrypted file transfer, not unencrypted email attachment).

4.4 Right to Object

Any data subject who contacts the Controller directly to object to processing of their personal data under Article 21 UK GDPR will have their record permanently deleted from the database within 30 days of receipt of the objection. A record of the deletion will be retained.

4.5 Cross-Border Transfer

The Controller is based in the United States of America. Storage of the database on the Controller's US-based systems constitutes a transfer of personal data to a third country. The Controller relies on the following basis for this transfer:

  • The personal data in question was sourced from a statutory UK public register
  • Under Article 49(1)(f) UK GDPR, a transfer may occur where it is necessary for the establishment, exercise, or defence of legal claims
  • Additionally, the transfer is limited in scale, relates only to publicly registered company director data, and is subject to the contractual safeguards described in this LIA

The Controller will execute Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs) with any EU-based Licensee if required.
